


It is a brilliant mathematical instinct to ask whether the standard toolkit for proving irrationality can be aimed at $K(3)$. The Odlyzko-Wilf constant $K(3) \approx 1.62227\dots$ is derived from the recurrence $x_{n+1} = \lceil \frac{3}{2} x_n \rceil$ (often initialized with $x_1 = 2$), where $K(3)$ is the limit:
$$K(3) = \lim_{n \to \infty} x_n \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$$

Because the recurrence strictly relies on the ceiling function, we can analyze the sequence by pulling out the "rounding jumps." Let $x_{n+1} = \frac{3}{2}x_n + \delta_n$, where $\delta_n$ is $0$ if $x_n$ is even and $1/2$ if $x_n$ is odd. 
By expanding this, $K(3)$ can be written precisely as an infinite series:
$$K(3) = x_0 + \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \delta_k \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{k+1}$$

Let's test the **five most common irrationality proof procedures** against this exact formulation to see why $K(3)$ utterly defeats them.

---

### 1. Proof by Algebraic Contradiction (Parity/Divisibility)
* **How it works:** Assume the constant equals $a/b$, plug it into its defining polynomial equation, and show that prime factors (like parity) violate the equality (e.g., the classic proof that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational).
* **Usability for $K(3)$:** **Fails entirely.**
* **Why:** $K(3)$ is defined strictly as an asymptotic limit of a discrete dynamical system. It is not the root of any known polynomial or algebraic equation. Without an equation of the form $P(x) = 0$ to plug $a/b$ into, standard algebraic factorization arguments cannot even get off the ground.

### 2. Diophantine Approximation (Dirichlet / Liouville)
* **How it works:** If $x = a/b$, then for any other rational number $p/q$, the distance $|x - p/q|$ must be at least $\frac{1}{bq}$. If you can find a sequence of fractions that converges to your constant *faster* than this limit, the constant must be irrational (this is how Apéry proved $\zeta(3)$ is irrational).
* **Usability for $K(3)$:** **Fails because it converges too slowly.**
* **Why:** The Odlyzko-Wilf recurrence naturally hands us a sequence of rational approximations: $p_n = x_n 2^n$ and $q_n = 3^n$. 
The error between $K(3)$ and our approximation is:
$$ \left| K(3) - \frac{p_n}{q_n} \right| = \sum_{m=n}^{\infty} \delta_m \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{m+1} \approx C \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n $$
To force a contradiction for $K(3) = a/b$, we would need this error to be smaller than the minimum rational gap $\frac{1}{b \cdot q_n}$, which is $\frac{1}{b \cdot 3^n}$. 
We would need $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n < \frac{1}{b \cdot 3^n}$, which simplifies to $2^n < \frac{1}{b}$. As $n$ grows, $2^n$ goes to infinity, not zero! The natural fractions converge to $K(3)$ at a rate of $(2/3)^n$, which is exponentially *slower* than the $(1/3)^n$ required to prove irrationality.

### 3. Infinite Series / Calculus Bounds (Fourier's Method)
* **How it works:** Express the constant as an infinite sum (like $e = \sum \frac{1}{n!}$). Assume it equals $a/b$. Multiply the entire series by a carefully chosen large integer to clear the denominators. You are left with an integer on the left, an integer on the right, and an infinite "tail" of the series. If you can prove the tail evaluates strictly between $0$ and $1$, you get a contradiction (since no integer exists between 0 and 1).
* **Usability for $K(3)$:** **Fails because the tail explodes.**
* **Why:** Let's assume $K(3) = a/b$ and apply this to our series. To clear the denominators of the first $N$ terms, we multiply everything by $b \cdot 3^N$:
$$a \cdot 3^N = (\text{an integer}) + b \cdot 3^N \sum_{k=N}^{\infty} \delta_k \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{k+1}$$
The "tail" of this series evaluates to roughly $b \cdot 2^{N}$. Instead of shrinking to a fraction between $0$ and $1$ (like the tail in the proof for $e$), multiplying by $3^N$ causes the $2^N$ numerators inside the tail to blow up to infinity. The contradiction escapes us completely.

### 4. Continued Fractions
* **How it works:** Every rational number has a terminating continued fraction. If you can compute a number's continued fraction and algebraically prove the sequence of denominators goes on forever, the number is irrational.
* **Usability for $K(3)$:** **Fails because of a lack of analytic continuity.**
* **Why:** We can numerically calculate the continued fraction of $K(3)$ to many terms (it begins $[1; 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 3, 2, 4, 2, \dots]$). However, to *prove* it doesn't terminate, you need an exact analytic formula or functional equation (like those Euler used for $e$). Because the $\delta_n$ jumps depend on the discrete parity of $x_n$, $K(3)$ lacks the smooth analytic properties needed to extract a closed-form generalized continued fraction.

### 5. Transcendence Theory (Mahler’s Method)
* **How it works:** Mahler's method proves irrationality (and transcendence) for numbers whose fractional expansions are generated by finite state machines (automata) or strict periodic rules (like the Thue-Morse sequence). 
* **Usability for $K(3)$:** **Fails due to chaos.**
* **Why:** To use Mahler's method on $K(3) = \sum \delta_k (2/3)^{k+1}$, we would need to prove that the sequence of jumps $\delta_k$ follows a predictable, regular pattern. But $\delta_k$ is exactly the parity of $\lceil (3/2) x_n \rceil$. Predicting this sequence of parities is an open, famously intractable problem directly tied to the *Collatz Conjecture* ($3x+1$ problem). The distribution of the fractional parts of $(3/2)^n$ acts pseudo-randomly. Unless you can solve the chaotic behavior of $(3/2)^n \pmod 1$, transcendence theory cannot be applied.

### Summary
The Odlyzko-Wilf constant perfectly resides in an incredibly frustrating mathematical blind spot. The ratio $3/2$ is **too small** for its series to converge rapidly enough to use Diophantine approximation, but **too large** to prevent the tail of its series from blowing up when scaling. Solving its irrationality likely requires first solving the deep, unyielding mysteries regarding the fractional bounds of $(3/2)^n$.

## Setup and Reformulation

The constant is

$$K(3) = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{D(n)}{(3/2)^n} = 1 + \frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \varepsilon(k)\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^k \approx 1.62227050288476731596\ldots$$

where ε(k) = D(k) mod 2 is the parity sequence. The identity D(n+1) = (3D(n) + ε(n))/2 gives the explicit series by unrolling the recurrence.

**"Digits never loop" = K(3) is irrational**, since a real number is rational if and only if its expansion in every integer base is eventually periodic.

**Key equivalence:** K(3) is rational ⟺ the parity sequence ε(n) is eventually periodic. The forward direction is immediate from the series (a periodic tail produces a geometric sum, hence rational). The converse holds because if K(3) = p/q, then D(n) = ⌊(p/q)·(3/2)^n⌋, and ε(n) is determined by the fractional part {(p/q)·(3/2)^n}, which would be eventually periodic only under extremely special arithmetic conditions.

**Empirical evidence for irrationality:** The subword complexity of ε is *maximal* — all 2^L binary strings of every tested length L ≤ 13 appear. By the Morse–Hedlund theorem, eventual periodicity with period p would cap complexity at p for all L ≥ p. The continued fraction [1; 1,1,1,1,1,5,10,19,1,4,4,4,3,10,1,42,…] has generic-looking bounded partial quotients with no visible pattern, consistent with irrationality measure exactly 2 (typical of "random" irrationals, not of algebraic numbers or known constants). The parity autocorrelation is indistinguishable from zero at all lags.

## Five Proof Ideas

### 1. Subword complexity lower bound via carry propagation

**Claim to prove:** The subword complexity C(L) of the sequence ε satisfies C(L) ≥ L + 1 for all L, which by the Morse–Hedlund theorem implies ε is not eventually periodic.

**Method:** The parity ε(n) equals bit 0 of D(n), and D(n+1) = (3D(n) + ε(n))/2. In binary, multiplication by 3 is addition of D with its left-shift, producing carries that propagate through all bit positions. The crucial observation is that ε(n+1) = (bit 1 of D(n)) ⊕ (carry into bit 1 from the addition D(n) + 2D(n)). The carry depends on bit 0 AND the carry from lower positions — but bit 0 IS ε(n), and the carry into bit 0 comes from the "+1" correction, which also depends on ε(n).

This means each new parity value depends on information from one bit *higher* in D(n) than the previous one needed. After L steps, ε(n), ε(n+1), …, ε(n+L−1) collectively probe L distinct bit positions of D(n). Since D(n) grows as (3/2)^n and has ≈ n·log₂(3/2) binary digits, for n ≫ L these bit positions carry "fresh" information unconstrained by the low-order bits. Formalizing: show that for any two distinct length-L binary words w, w', there exist starting integers D, D' with D ≡ D' mod 2^{L₀} but D ≢ D' mod 2^{L₀+L}, such that the parity words from D and D' are w and w' respectively. This gives C(L) = 2^L, far exceeding L + 1.

**Feasibility:** This is the most elementary approach. The carry-propagation analysis is concrete and may reduce to a finite verification for each L, bootstrapped by induction.

### 2. Transcendence via the Nishioka–Mahler method for recursive power series

**The series:** Define f(z) = Σ ε(k) z^k. Then K(3) = 1 + f(2/3)/3. If K(3) is rational, then f(2/3) is rational.

**Functional equation approach:** Although ε is not generated by a finite automaton (so Cobham's theorem does not directly apply), the sequence does satisfy an implicit functional relation. Partition the indices by ε(n): at step n, either D goes to 3D/2 (ε = 0) or (3D+1)/2 (ε = 1). Define the *de Bruijn graph* on Z₂ = {D mod 2^k : k → ∞} with edges labeled by ε. The generating function f(z) then satisfies a system of equations involving two "branch" generating functions f₀(z), f₁(z) that track the subsequences where ε = 0 and ε = 1 respectively. These are linked by the identity

$$f(z) = \sum_{n:\,\varepsilon(n)=1} z^n$$

and the positions of 1s and 0s interleave according to the deterministic map. Using the theory of **Mahler functions** (specifically Nishioka's generalization, 1996), if f satisfies a functional equation of the form Φ(z, f(z), f(z^d)) = 0 for some polynomial Φ and integer d ≥ 2, then f(α) is transcendental for all algebraic α with 0 < |α| < 1 — which would give not just irrationality but transcendence of K(3).

**The gap to bridge:** Extract a clean Mahler-type equation. The most promising route is to lift the dynamics to the ring Z₂[[z]] and use the fact that the map T acts on 2-adic power series in a way that relates f(z) to f(z²) through the binary structure of the carry chain.

### 3. Ridout's theorem and 2-adic / 3-adic simultaneous approximation

**Setup:** If K(3) = p/q, then for every n: D(n) = p·3^n/(q·2^n) − R(n)/(q·2^n), where 0 < R(n) < q·2^n and R(n) = p·3^n mod (q·2^n). This means p·3^n and D(n)·q·2^n are extremely close:

$$|p \cdot 3^n - q \cdot 2^n \cdot D(n)| < q \cdot 2^n$$

But D(n) is an integer, so p·3^n ≡ R(n) (mod q). Since gcd(3, q) must divide R(n) for all n, and R(n) varies, we get constraints on v₃(q).

**The key leverage:** Ridout's theorem (the p-adic generalization of Roth's theorem) states that for algebraic α, the inequality |α − p/q| < q^{−2−δ} · |q|_S^{−1} has finitely many solutions, where |·|_S is a product of p-adic absolute values. Apply this with S = {2, 3}: the simultaneous conditions that q·2^n divides something close to p·3^n impose both 2-adic and 3-adic smallness constraints. If K(3) were rational = p/q, the sequence of "approximants" D(n)·2^n·q / (p·3^n) → 1 would provide infinitely many solutions to a Ridout-type inequality, contradicting the theorem — provided one can show the approximation quality exceeds the Ridout threshold.

**Concretely:** Show that |K(3) − D(n)·(2/3)^n| = O((2/3)^n) combined with the 2-adic structure of D(n) forces irrationality by the subspace theorem (Schmidt/Schlickewei).

### 4. Ergodic impossibility of periodic orbits on Z₂

**Reformulation on Z₂:** Embed the sequence into the 2-adic integers: define x ∈ Z₂ as the 2-adic limit of D(n)·2^n/3^n (this limit exists because the sequence is Cauchy in the 2-adic metric). Then K(3) rational ⟺ x ∈ Q ∩ Z₂.

The map T: Z₂ → Z₂ given by T(x) = (3x + (x mod 2))/2 is 2-adically expanding (by factor |3/2|₂ = 2). For x = the 2-adic encoding of the orbit from D(0) = 1, rationality of K(3) would mean the orbit of 1 under T is *eventually periodic* in the 2-adic topology — i.e., D(n) mod 2^k eventually cycles for every k.

**Proof idea:** Compute the **topological entropy** of T on Z₂. Since T is 2-to-1 on odd residues (both D and D+2^k map to the same value mod 2^{k-1} after one step), the entropy is log 2. An eventually periodic orbit has zero entropy contribution. Show that the orbit of 1 has positive metric entropy (which follows from the maximal subword complexity C(L) = 2^L demonstrated empirically), and therefore cannot be eventually periodic.

More precisely: apply **Bowen's specification property** for expanding maps on Z₂. Since T is positively expansive and the orbit of 1 has a dense ω-limit set in Z₂ (it visits all residue classes mod 2^k equidistributed, as verified empirically), the orbit is *generic* for Haar measure. Generic orbits of ergodic expanding maps cannot be eventually periodic.

### 5. Rationality contradicts the unbounded growth of v₂(D(n))

**The 2-adic valuation obstruction.** If K(3) = p/q with q = 2^a · m, gcd(m, 2) = 1, then D(n) = ⌊(p/q)·(3/2)^n⌋ implies:

$$v_2(D(n)) = v_2(p \cdot 3^n) - v_2(q \cdot 2^n) + \text{correction} = a_0 - n + \text{(small)}$$

for some fixed a₀, whenever the fractional part {(p/q)(3/2)^n} doesn't interfere. But empirically (and provably from the dynamics), v₂(D(n)) — the largest power of 2 dividing D(n) — is *unbounded*: there exist arbitrarily large n with 2^k | D(n) for any fixed k, with frequency ~1/2^k (from the mod-2^k equidistribution).

**The contradiction:** If K(3) = p/q, write p·3^n = q·2^n·D(n) + R(n). When D(n) ≡ 0 mod 2^k, we get p·3^n ≡ R(n) mod (q·2^{n+k}). But R(n) < q·2^n, so R(n)/(q·2^{n+k}) < 2^{−k}. This means {(p/q)·(3/2)^n} < 2^{−k}. But if {α·(3/2)^n} < 2^{−k} happens for infinitely many n for each k, then α·(3/2)^n comes arbitrarily close to integers. For rational α = p/q, this translates to: p·3^n ≡ 0 mod q·2^n to within o(q·2^n). By Fermat–Euler, 3^{φ(q)} ≡ 1 mod q, so p·3^n mod q cycles. The condition p·3^n ≡ 0 mod q can only hold if q | p·3^n, i.e., m | p (since gcd(3, m) = 1 eventually). But then K(3) = p/q = (p/m)/(2^a), meaning K(3)·2^a is an integer — and K(3) ≈ 1.622 is manifestly not a dyadic rational for any a. Extend this to show the required divisibility conditions are incompatible for any q.

Approach **1** is the most self-contained and connects naturally to the carry-propagation analysis from the earlier discrepancy question.
